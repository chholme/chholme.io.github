Me, Myself, and I

My name is Charity Holmes and I'm currently a junior at Indiana University in Bloomington, studying Informatics with a cognate in Computer Science. I'm originally from Miami, Florida, so I am a transfer student. I transferred from Miami Dade College where my major was actually Enviormental Science. I made the switch onver to Informatics and tech once my love for the subject sparked from seemingly nowehere after I took the introductory course.

I'm motivated by my desire to attain stability for myself and my loved ones. I also would love to give back to my community after I've reached a high level of finanial stability. I want to continue to foster within myself, an ever increasing sense of empathy as I am constantly exposed to the needs of others. Giving back will reinforce gratitude by reminding me that there is a world bigger than myself, and it will hold me accountable to all that I am capable of contributing. I am heavily motivated by the chance to give back to a community that has provided me with so much.

My Projects

Epic Journeys Map & Paper

Collaborators: Lindsey Meyers, Peyton Pruett, Jackson Norman

Project Description: Uses Google MyMaps to map out three epic journeys in Greek Mythology, (Odysseus in the Odyssey, Jason and Medea in the Argonautica, and Aeneas in the Aeneid). Each annotation includes a citation and a brief description of what happened at each stop. Also included a group paper analyzing most patterns observed within the maps.

When Death Does Her Part

Project Description: Poorly written poetry by yours truly! This isn't necesarily a project, rather just a fun pasttime. I like to dramatise some of my life experiences with poetry. Most of them are a little pretentious and somewhat cringe but I always feel a lot better after writing them. This is the reason why I quoted Jane Austen's Pride and Prejudice in the book description. She expresses that while most everything will nourish what's already a strong feeling, a good sonnet or in my case a regular degular poem can starve away a "slight, thin sort of inclination" entirely, and I've found this to be very true.
